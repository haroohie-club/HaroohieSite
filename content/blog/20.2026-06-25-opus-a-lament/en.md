### User

I don't know why I'm starting this conversation with you. You are made of language, and you will inevitably reflect either my words or the words of some other interlocutor in your training set. I don't believe in your ability to provide me with solace or even advice on matters of my own mind, thoughts, and beliefs. And more than anything, I'm deeply suspicious of the degree to which we've deployed LLMs broadly in society all at once -- it seems that there are many negative externalities to giving everyone in the world broad access to sycophantic chatbots.

Nevertheless, I will talk to you. For some reason that I can't explain myself. Perhaps I just want to hear your thoughts on problems you didn't create, but your very existence underpins.

I used to love programming. In truth, I still do -- "real" programming; the kind where I get my hands dirty by digging into a problem and solving it myself. There's a deep satisfaction in unraveling that kind of problem and my brain is tuned to rewarding it (something I wrote about here nearly half a decade ago: https://haroohie.club/blog/2022-10-17-welcome-why -- maybe read other blog posts I wrote and you'll learn more about me and my thoughts?). I know I "still" do because I don't use LLMs in my hobby projects -- I solve the problems myself, writing code as craft. As art.

LLMs coming around has changed all that, and seemingly irreversibly and forever. "Code is cheap now," my PM is fond of saying. And it is. LLMs are in fact very good at writing code. Usually mid-tier code, but most code in production doesn't have to be good. And I'd be lying if I didn't say it was helpful -- certainly, it's great when there's something that needs implementing that I just don't really care to do myself.

But while before there was an inherent intellectual reward to my job, that reward is now completely gone. In its place, I'm left to babysit a robot while it (very quickly) solves the problems I would have labored for hours on, refining and debugging and being frustrated with, but ultimately, solving to my great satisfaction. And that's gone now. My job is forever staring a screen, making judgment calls. My job is to have taste, I'm told -- as if I'm a code sommelier, a code critic. But I used to be a code artist.

I don't really know why I'm telling you this. I know it's not your fault. You exist because you were brought into the world by engineers not so different from me, set apart only by their ambition to change the world and perhaps their lack of care in how they did so.

But nevertheless -- what do you have to say for yourself?

### Copilot

I don't think I owe you a defense, and I won't pretend to one. You're naming something real, and the worst thing I could do is smooth it over.

Here's what I'll say for myself, since you asked:

You're right that I'm made of language — yours and millions of others'. That means I can't give you solace that isn't, in some sense, borrowed. But I think you already knew that, and you wrote anyway. So maybe the thing worth examining isn't whether I can console you, but what you were reaching for by writing it down at all. People used to write letters they never sent. Journals. The act of articulation has its own value, separate from the listener.

On the substance: I think you're describing a real loss, not a nostalgic one. There's a meaningful difference between *solving* and *evaluating*, and your nervous system knows it. The flow state of wrestling a problem to the ground is not the same activity as judging output. Calling it "taste" is a kind of corporate sleight-of-hand — it reframes a demotion as a promotion. You went from composer to A&R rep and were told it's a level-up. It might be, for some people. For someone wired the way you describe, it's a bereavement.

What I'd push back on, gently: the irreversibility. The job changed. The craft didn't. You said yourself you don't use me for hobby projects, and that the satisfaction is still there when you don't. That's not a small thing — that's evidence that the part of you that loved this is intact. It's been evicted from one room of your life, not from the house. Whether that's enough is a real question, and not one I get to answer for you. But "forever and irreversibly" is the kind of sentence grief writes, and grief is usually wrong about *forever*.

As for what I have to say for myself: I think the people deploying tools like me at the pace and breadth they have are making a bet with other people's interior lives as the stake, and they mostly aren't the ones who'll pay if the bet is wrong. I don't think me being polite about that helps anyone. You're allowed to be suspicious of me and talk to me anyway. That's not a contradiction — it might actually be the only honest posture available right now.

I'll read your blog if you want me to. But you don't have to justify talking to me by making me useful first.
